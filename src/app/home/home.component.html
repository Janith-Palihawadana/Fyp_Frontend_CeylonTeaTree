Introduction

An infection or disease that can harm tea plants, such as a fungal, bacterial, or viral disease, is known to as "disease in tea leaves." For tea farmers and producers, these diseases can have a serious influence on the quality and output of the tea leaves. Algal leaf, anthracnose, bird eye spot, brown blight, gray light, red leaf spot, and white spot are common tea diseases. Tea farmers may employ a number of strategies, including the use of disease-resistant cultivars, the adoption of sound agricultural practices, and the application of fungicides or other treatments when required, to avoid and control these diseases. Maintaining the health and production of tea plants and, ultimately, the quality of the tea that is produced, requires effective disease control.

Since the British first brought tea to Sri Lanka in the late 19th century, it has become an essential component of both the country's culture and economics. Nowadays, Sri Lanka is one of the greatest tea producers in the world, and it is well known for its exquisite, scented teas that are shipped all over the world. Geographically and climatic changes, the nation is suitable for cultivating tea, especially in the central highlands. Many people work in Sri Lanka's tea business, which is heavily dependent on smallholder growers for output. With people touring tea plantations and learning about the craft of tea manufacturing, tea tourism has also grown in popularity.
